# NIST AI Risk Management Framework Study Corpus

This compact corpus is derived from the public NIST AI Risk Management Framework topic structure. It is written as a study corpus for testing RAG evaluation behavior and avoids employer data.

## Govern Function

The Govern function establishes organizational policies, roles, responsibilities, and accountability practices for trustworthy AI. It emphasizes documentation, risk tolerance, stakeholder engagement, lifecycle oversight, and clear escalation paths. Governance activities connect risk management to leadership decisions rather than treating risk controls as only technical checks.

## Map Function

The Map function frames the AI system's intended purpose, context, stakeholders, benefits, assumptions, and potential harms. Mapping identifies who may be affected, where the system will operate, and which risks are relevant in the deployment environment. It makes downstream measurement and management meaningful by defining context first.

## Measure Function

The Measure function evaluates identified risks through qualitative and quantitative methods. It includes testing, evaluation, validation, documentation of uncertainty, impact assessment, and monitoring. Measurement should address performance and also trustworthy characteristics such as robustness, fairness, explainability, privacy, and safety.

## Manage Function

The Manage function prioritizes, responds to, and monitors AI risks after they have been mapped and measured. It includes mitigation, acceptance, transfer, avoidance, incident response, and continuous monitoring. Management decisions should be traceable to documented risk criteria.

## Valid and Reliable

An AI system is valid and reliable when it performs as intended for its stated context and maintains acceptable performance across expected operating conditions. Validation and reliability evidence should include representative testing, monitoring, and awareness of limitations.

## Safe

Safety means the AI system should not create unacceptable physical, psychological, economic, environmental, or societal harm under expected use and reasonably foreseeable misuse. Safety controls should consider users, affected communities, and the operational environment.

## Secure and Resilient

Secure and resilient AI systems can resist, respond to, and recover from attacks, failures, or unexpected operating conditions. Security includes confidentiality, integrity, availability, and robustness against adversarial manipulation.

## Accountable and Transparent

Accountability and transparency require traceable decisions, clear documentation, meaningful explanations, and assigned responsibility for outcomes. Transparency should be appropriate for the audience and purpose, including users, developers, auditors, and affected stakeholders.

## Explainable and Interpretable

Explainability and interpretability help people understand why an AI system produced a result and whether the result can be trusted. The needed level of explanation depends on context, risk, user expertise, and decision impact.

## Privacy Enhanced

Privacy-enhanced AI protects data from inappropriate collection, exposure, inference, retention, and reuse. Privacy controls may include minimization, access limits, secure processing, and evaluation of privacy risks throughout the lifecycle.

## Fair with Harmful Bias Managed

Fairness work identifies and manages harmful bias that can lead to inequitable outcomes. Bias management includes representative data practices, subgroup evaluation, impact assessment, stakeholder feedback, and mitigation of unjustified disparities.

## Documentation and Traceability

AI risk management benefits from documentation that links assumptions, data choices, model behavior, test results, limitations, and mitigation actions. Traceability supports audits, maintenance, incident review, and communication across teams.

## Continuous Monitoring

AI systems can drift as data, users, or environments change. Continuous monitoring tracks performance, safety, security, fairness, and other risk indicators after deployment. Monitoring should trigger review when thresholds or risk conditions change.

## Human Oversight

Human oversight assigns people meaningful roles in reviewing, contesting, approving, or stopping AI system behavior. Oversight should be designed around actual user capabilities and should not be a symbolic control with no practical effect.

## Third-Party Risk

Organizations using external models, datasets, tools, or services still need to manage risks from those components. Third-party risk practices include supplier documentation, contractual expectations, testing, monitoring, and contingency plans.
